Introduction

A quick showcase of what I do and who I am

I'm a South African who recently moved to Dublin to join Demonware as a Site Reliablity Engineer. I've spent the past two years helping them redefine what it means to build and monitor business critical, customer facing services at scale. Over that period, I've been instrumental in helping them transition from a NOC-centric model to an organization with no NOC and better KPIs across the board for it.

I've been writing customer facing code for the past decade and my experience ranges from C# desktop applications with tens of customers to realtime, web based GIS and data analytics packages and most recently, the monitoring tooling we use at Demonware to support hundreds of millions of monthly active users. All this has been accomplished with a plethora of languages and frameworks, with the goal of using the right tool for each job. Everything from JavaScript and Python to C/C++ and Go with a range of other esoteric choices thrown in for spice.

While much of my time and career has been devoted to the development of software, my initial exposure to programming came at the hands of Drupal and this naturally led to me taking up the reins of a SysAdmin. Over the years I continued to run my own infrastructure both for my own personal projects as well as for the various jobs I had. Everything from Ubuntu and CentOS VPS's to Windows Server installations and CoreOS clusters. When it became clear that manually provisioning and configuring those machines wasn't sustainable, I adopted Ansible and proceeded to build comprehensive infrastructure-as-code repositories which allowed me to use CI/CD to deploy my infrastructure and applications.

More recently, I've been working to transition Demonware's Monitoring team to a fully containerized environment, where we leverage Kubernetes, Jenkins, GitFlow and Helm to provide CI/CDelivery for every one of our 50+ microservices. The result is that in many ways, we are years ahead of any of the true software engineering teams in both agility and deployment reliability.

In addition to my professional experience, I've also spent years contributing to various open source projects and even developing my own. I thoroughly enjoy the opportunity to contribute to the community that helped me develop the skills I have today and take an active role in mentoring anybody who displays an interest.

When I'm not programming, the same drive to constantly challenge myself and push the boundary of what is possible has led me to take up a range of hobbies from Hiking and Rock Climbing to Scuba Diving and Adeventure Motorcycling. If I haven't done it yet, I'm probably planning on it. Of course, the cautious side of my nature means that I've regularly been accused of entirely missing the point of doing "adrenaline junkie" hobbies.

What I did at EMSS

I joined EMSS as a software engineer on their IXUS team, where my responsibilities primarily focussed on maintaining the backend application stack for IXUS, their RF safety certification suite used by various cellular network providers around the world.

Over my tenure there, I was responsible for a number of projects ranging from frontend web applications, hardware communication protocols, massively parallelizable data import pipelines and self-healing application clusters.

While a large portion of my legacy there involves the projects I developed while working at EMSS, perhaps my biggest contribution was driving the deployment and adoption of GitLab as a replacement for the company's legacy AccuRev source control system. By demonstrating not only the ability to port some of their most complex application stacks to a full CI/CD pipeline, but also the value in doing so, I was able to overcome the familiarity that their most senior engineers had with AccuRev and convince them to drive the adoption on their own teams.

My projects at EMSS

While my original responsibilities involved maintaining a legacy application stack, I quickly moved onto developing new tools and services at EMSS. This started with a rewritten antenna model portal which allowed customers to search for and download the electromagnetic models for various cellular antennas. I worked to build a dynamic expression generator which enabled structured language parsing into semantically correct and highly optimized SQL queries and managed to cut the search time for antennas from some 20 seconds to fewer than 20 milliseconds on average.

I was also involved in the development of a low-level USB communication library in C++ (and accompanying UI) to extract RF exposure information from FieldSense, a personal exposure monitor that EMSS sells.

Perhaps my most interesting project at EMSS was the development of a realtime GIS platform for the South African agricultural market which enabled farmers to track the efficiency of pesticide spraying on their farms through a central web portal. This was an incredibly complex project and there's nowhere near enough space here to do it justice, however I am more than happy to talk about it if you're interested.

What is Sierra Softworks?

Sierra Softworks is my personal company, started while I was in High School, it served to give me brand which I could cultivate for the various pieces of software I developed and released over the years.

Some of the most valuable lessons I've learnt over the past 8 years of running Sierra Softworks have nothing to do with technology. Rather; they involve the interactions with customers that have shaped the development of software, requested support for projects I had long forgotten about or asked for advice on their own projects.

From these interactions, I've shaped many of the processes and priorities that are integral to my development today. These include a strong tendency to drive for an AGILE style of software development, heavy emphasis on repeatable and automated builds, a no-compromises view on the need for refactoring and managing of tech-debt and comprehensive runtime instrumentation.
